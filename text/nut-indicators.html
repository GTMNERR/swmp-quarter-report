5.1 Summary

Dissolved oxygen dropped and was lower than average from late January through mid-March and again during the hot month of July at Pine Island and Pellicer Creek (Figure 5.1 (a); Figure 5.1 (b)). This pattern is likely correlated with the warmer than average temperatures this area experienced during this time (Figure 4.2; Figure 4.3). Waters at all stations were oxygenated following spring rainfall events and Nor’easters (Figure 4.1 (c); Figure 5.1). Pellicer Creek had daily average dissolved oxygen values that fell within normal ranges (Figure 5.1 (d)), however low dissolved oxygen values were still collected at the site in July (Figure 5.2). Pine Island has experienced very low dissolved oxygen values (less than 2 mg/L) frequently in the past two years, which is uncharacteristically low for this station, particularly in the past five years (Figure 5.3).

Chlorophyll a values were higher than average at all sites (Figure 5.4), frequently over the thresholds at each site, particularly at San Sebastian and Pellicer Creek (Figure 5.4 (b), Figure 5.4 (d)). Although too soon to make inferences on the patterns in the annual geometric means (AGM), so far the data shows elevated AGM chlorophyll a at all sites except Fort Matanzas (Figure 5.5).

Interpretations of patterns in total nitrogen (TN) and dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN) are difficult this year since quite a bit of the values were not able to be calculated. Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen (TKN) was lower than average for most sites however all sites experienced higher levels in May (Figure 5.8).

Total phosphorus continues to fall below the state threshold for every station and similar to TKN, all sites experienced elevated values of total phosphorus and orthophosphate in May (Figure 5.9; Figure 5.10) except for Pine Island which had higher levels for almost every month (Figure 5.10 (a)).

5.2 Data

5.2.1 Dissolved Oxygen

Pellicer Creek is located within a waterbody with known impairments for dissolved oxygen, chlorophyll a, and fecal coliform bacteria. As such, this station frequently experiences low dissolved oxygen values, particularly in the summer months (Figure 5.2).

Dissolved oxygen at Pine Island has been uncharacteristically low in the past two years.

5.2.2 Chlorophyll a

At the conclusion of 2023, variability and patterns in the monthly chlorophyll a data will be examined by decomposing the timeseries at each station using Equation 4 from Cloern and Jassby (2010) into an annual effect, mean seasonal pattern, and residual “events”.

Equation 4 from Cloern and Jassby (2010):

\[ c_{ij}=Cy_im_j\epsilon_{ij} \] Where \(c_{ij}\) is chlorophyll concentration in year \(i\)(\(i=1,...,N\)) and month \(j\)(\(j=1,...12\)); C is the long-term mean of the series; \(y_i\) is the annual effect in the ith year; \(m_j\) is the seasonal (monthly) effect in the jth month; and \(\epsilon_{ij}\) is the residual.

  • If \(y_i\) >1, indicates a year with above-average mean Chl-a.
  • If \(m_j\) >1, indicates a mean monthly Chl-a greater than the annual mean.
  • If \(\epsilon_{ij}\) >1, indicates an observation greater than the expected value for that month and year.

5.2.3 Nitrogen

5.2.3.1 Total Nitrogen

TN is a calculated value based on TKN and NO23.

Several of the NO23 values fell below the Florida Department of Environmental Protection Central Lab’s minimum detection limit and therefore TN was not able to be calculated and is missing for quite a few months at all the stations (Figure 5.6). This was especially true at Fort Matanzas which has the least amount of TN data (Figure 5.6 (c)).

5.2.3.2 Dissolved Inorganic Nitrogen

DIN is a calculated value based on NO23 and ammonia (NH4). Due to the low or undetectable levels of NO23, DIN is also missing quite a bit of information (Figure 5.7).

5.2.3.3 Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen

TKN is the total concentration of organic nitrogen and ammonia. It is directly measured in our samples.
